**Starting an IT career is achievable without a prior technical background, but it requires a deliberate sequence of steps.** The professionals who break in fastest are those who stack one credential, one skill, and one experience on top of each other rather than trying to master everything at once.

*The most common mistake beginners make is spending months studying without ever applying for a job. Start applying earlier than feels comfortable.*

## Step 1: Understand the Field and Choose a Direction

Before you study anything, decide which part of IT interests you most. You do not need to commit forever, but a general direction focuses your learning.

| Starting Point | What to Learn First |
|----------------|---------------------|
| **Help Desk / Desktop Support** | Windows, hardware, Active Directory basics |
| **Networking** | CompTIA Network+, Cisco CCNA |
| **Systems / Sysadmin** | Linux, Windows Server, virtualization |
| **Cloud** | AWS Cloud Practitioner or Azure AZ-900 |
| **Security** | CompTIA Security+ (after A+ and Network+) |

If you have no direction yet, start with Help Desk. It is the broadest entry point and teaches troubleshooting fundamentals that apply everywhere.

## Step 2: Earn Your First Certification

**CompTIA A+** is the standard first certification for IT professionals. It covers:

- Hardware components and troubleshooting
- Operating systems (Windows, macOS, Linux basics)
- Mobile devices and printing
- Networking fundamentals
- Security concepts
- Operational procedures

The A+ exam consists of two parts (Core 1: 220-1201, Core 2: 220-1202). Study time is typically 60–120 hours for a complete beginner. Professor Messer's free video course is the standard study resource.

*Do not wait until you feel "ready" to schedule the exam. Set an exam date 6–8 weeks out from when you start studying. The deadline creates focus.*

## Step 3: Build a Home Lab

A certification proves knowledge. A home lab proves skill. Employers notice the difference.

A basic home lab requires very little investment:

- A single PC capable of running a hypervisor (8 GB RAM minimum, 16 GB recommended)
- [Proxmox VE](https://www.proxmox.com/) (free) or VMware Workstation (paid) for virtualization
- A used managed switch ($30–$50 on eBay) for networking practice
- Free cloud accounts (AWS, Azure, GCP all offer free tiers)

Run a Windows Server VM. Set up Active Directory. Create users. Break something and fix it. Document everything.

See the full guide: [Building an IT Home Lab](/it-career-playbook/getting-started-in-it/building-an-it-home-lab/)

## Step 4: Get Your First IT Role

Getting the first job is the hardest step. Strategies that work:

- **Apply to help desk and IT support roles aggressively.** Apply even when you meet only 60–70% of the listed requirements.
- **Target Managed Service Providers (MSPs).** MSPs hire junior technicians constantly and expose you to many environments quickly.
- **Use staffing agencies.** IT staffing firms (Insight Global, TEKsystems, Robert Half Technology) regularly place contractors in entry-level IT support roles that convert to full-time.
- **Consider government IT.** Federal, state, and local government IT agencies hire at genuine entry-level with defined career ladders.

Your resume should list your certifications prominently, include a home lab section describing what you built, and quantify any prior experience (even non-IT experience that shows reliability and problem-solving).

## Step 5: Stack Your Credentials While Working

Once you are in your first role, the path to advancement accelerates:

1. Earn **CompTIA Network+** while doing help desk (shows networking depth)
2. Earn **CompTIA Security+** (now a baseline expectation for many IT roles)
3. Move into a sysadmin or cloud engineering role
4. Earn an intermediate certification in your chosen specialization
