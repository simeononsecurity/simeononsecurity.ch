**The short answer: a degree helps but is not strictly required.** Software development is one of the few high-paying professional fields where employers regularly hire people without a four-year degree, based instead on demonstrated skills, portfolio work, and the ability to pass a technical interview. *What matters most to most employers is: can you build software, can you work in a team, and can you learn when requirements change?*

The longer answer depends on where you want to work, what you want to build, and how much time and money you are willing to invest.

## What a Computer Science Degree Actually Provides

A CS degree from an accredited university gives you:

| What You Get | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Algorithms and data structures** | Foundational knowledge for technical interviews and systems design |
| **Operating systems and computer architecture** | Necessary for systems, embedded, and performance-critical work |
| **Mathematics (discrete math, linear algebra, probability)** | Essential for ML/AI, graphics, cryptography, and algorithm design |
| **Networking fundamentals** | Relevant for backend, DevOps, and security engineering |
| **Software engineering principles** | Code design, testing, version control discipline |
| **Credential that unlocks certain doors** | Some employers filter applications by degree |
| **Internship pipeline access** | University career fairs and recruiting programs that self-taught devs do not have |

The degree is most valuable for the first job search and for roles at companies that screen by education. Its relative importance drops significantly after you have 3 to 5 years of professional experience.

## When a CS Degree Is Worth Pursuing

- You are early in your career with time and affordable access to a program.
- You want to work at FAANG or comparable companies that have historically required CS degrees.
- You are interested in ML, AI, computer graphics, cryptography, or systems programming where math and theory depth matters.
- You want access to research roles or PhD programs.
- You are pursuing a DoD or cleared government position where education carries more weight.

## When You Can Skip the Degree

- You need to be earning within 12 months (family obligations, debt, career change).
- The cost of the program would create debt that does not pencil out on a developer salary timeline.
- You are targeting web development, mobile, or DevOps roles where portfolio evidence dominates hiring decisions.
- You already have a degree in another field and bring domain expertise (finance, healthcare, education) that you can combine with development skills.

## The Real Filter: Technical Interviews

For most developer roles, the hiring filter is the technical interview, not the resume screen for education. A candidate who can solve algorithm problems, explain system design decisions, write clean code, and communicate their thinking will outperform a credentialed candidate who cannot.

The path to surviving technical interviews requires:

1. Data structures and algorithm fundamentals (linked lists, trees, graphs, hash maps, sorting).
2. Practice on LeetCode, HackerRank, or similar platforms.
3. A portfolio that demonstrates real projects.
4. The ability to explain code and design decisions clearly.

*None of these require a degree. All of them require time and discipline.*

## The Middle Path: Community College and Associate Degrees

A two-year associate degree in computer science or information technology from an accredited community college provides:

- Structured fundamentals at roughly 10 to 20% of the cost of a four-year program.
- Transferable credits to a four-year program if you later want to complete a bachelor's.
- A credential that satisfies education requirements at many employers.
- A realistic timeline of 18 to 24 months to first job-ready status.

This is worth considering if cost is the barrier to a four-year degree but you want the credential.
