**A software engineering internship is the highest-leverage career investment you can make as a student or early-career developer.** Full-time conversion rates at top tech companies from internship are 60 to 80%. An internship provides professional experience, a network of peers, and a reference that cold applications cannot replicate. *If you can get an internship, prioritize it over any other credential.*

## Types of Developer Internships

| Type | Typical Duration | Pay | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Summer internship (university)** | 10–12 weeks | $30–$60/hr at top companies | Students entering junior or senior year |
| **Co-op program** | 6–12 months alternating with school | $25–$55/hr | Extended immersion; common at Northeastern, Drexel, Georgia Tech |
| **Part-time internship** | Semester-length | $20–$40/hr | Students who cannot pause enrollment |
| **Bootcamp externship** | 4–8 weeks | Unpaid or low-pay | Bootcamp grads building experience |
| **Open source fellowship** | 3 months | Stipend ($3K–$6K) | Google Summer of Code, Outreachy, MLH Fellowship |

| Source | Notes |

## What Interviewers Look for in Intern Candidates

Intern interviews are typically lighter than full-time interviews. Expectations by level:

**For freshmen/sophomores:**
- Data structures basics: arrays, linked lists, stacks, queues.
- One programming language fluency (Python or Java preferred).
- Ability to write a simple function without hints.

**For juniors/seniors:**
- Two or three LeetCode medium-level problems.
- Basic system design awareness (not full system design interviews, but "how would you store this data?" questions).
- A portfolio project to discuss.

*Start preparing for internship interviews in October for summer programs. Large companies start opening applications in August and September for the following summer.*

## The Internship Application Timeline

| Month | Action |
|---|---|
| **August–September** | Top companies (Google, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, Apple) open summer internship applications |
| **October–November** | Mid-market tech companies open applications; apply aggressively |
| **December–January** | Attend university career fairs; final applications for most large companies |
| **February–March** | Smaller companies still have openings; keep applying |
| **March–April** | Most offers are distributed; accept by deadline |

*Apply early. Companies hire from the top of the application pool, and pools close once headcount is filled.*

## Performing Well During Your Internship

Getting the internship is not the goal. The goal is converting it to a full-time offer or a strong reference.

- **Deliver your project.** The number one failure mode is scope creep and not shipping. Scope down early, ship on time.
- **Ask for feedback at the midpoint, not the end.** A midpoint check-in allows you to correct course while it still matters.
- **Communicate blockers immediately.** An internship manager expects blockers. Silence with no output is the worst outcome.
- **Network broadly.** Lunch with engineers outside your team. Attending intern events. Your intern cohort is your strongest professional network for the next decade.
- **Document your work.** A well-written design doc or final presentation makes your return offer case visible beyond just your mentor.

## Converting to a Full-Time Offer

Most companies extend return offers based on direct observation and your mentor's recommendation. Factors that drive the offer:

- Project completion and quality.
- Code review performance (did you respond well to feedback?).
- Team fit and communication.
- Manager and mentor advocacy.

If you receive a return offer: evaluate it as a real offer with market comparison, not as a guaranteed first job you must take.
